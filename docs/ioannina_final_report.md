# Ioannina 12 MW Data Center Strategy Report

## Project and objective

This report evaluates three strategic options for a **single 12 MW IT load data center in Ioannina**:

1. **Turnkey**
2. **Powered Shell**
3. **Build-to-exit**

The purpose is to recommend the best option for the sponsor based on:
- assumptions and market-backed modeling basis,
- base / downside / upside scenario results,
- SWOT,
- risk analysis,
- Monte Carlo output,
- decision matrix,
- and a board-level conclusion.

**Important note:** all financial results below are **underwriting model outputs**, not market facts. They depend on the assumptions stated here and should be updated when real EPC budgets, lender terms, tenant pricing feedback, and utility milestones are available.

---

## 1. Executive summary

### Recommended option now
**Powered Shell** is the best current option for the Ioannina project.

### Why
- It requires the **lowest capital at risk**.
- It gives the **lowest break-even rent** and a more forgiving break-even occupancy.
- It preserves **optionality** in a market that is strategically credible but not yet publicly proven as a deep 12 MW wholesale cloud-region market.
- It performs best on a **risk-adjusted** basis in the model.

### Second-best option
**Build-to-exit** is attractive only if management explicitly wants a **3 to 4 year capital recycling strategy** and accepts higher sensitivity to exit yield, sale timing, and lease-up.

### Third-best option
**Speculative Turnkey** should not be the lead strategy unless the sponsor first secures a **strong anchor pre-let** with bankable covenant quality.

### Main strategic conclusion
The project should be positioned as:

> **Powered Shell with pre-engineered Turnkey convertibility**

That means the sponsor should preserve the ability to convert the scheme to Turnkey if a credible anchor emerges, but should not commit full speculative Turnkey capex from day one.

## 3. Modeling basis

### 3.1 Market and pricing logic
The modeling logic reflects the following high-level market view:
- prime leased data centers in the deepest institutional markets trade at much tighter yields than secondary or thinner markets,
- speed-to-power and pre-leasing have become more important as European capacity has tightened,
- speculative capex is more dangerous in thinner markets,
- therefore Ioannina should be underwritten as a **credible secondary digital node**, not as a prime cloud-region benchmark.

### 3.2 Why the three options are different

#### Turnkey
The owner funds major fit-out and MEP scope, delivers a nearly tenant-ready product, and seeks higher rent at much higher capex.

#### Powered Shell
The owner delivers building, power, and mechanical backbone but leaves more tenant scope to the occupier. This lowers capex and raises flexibility.

#### Build-to-exit
This is treated as an **ownership strategy**, not a product type. The model assumes a shell-led development that is leased up and sold in 2031.

### 3.3 Valuation logic
The exit or terminal value is based on:

**Exit value = Next year NOI / Exit yield**

This means value depends mainly on:
- stabilized NOI,
- and the yield the market would require for that type of asset.

### 3.4 Financing caveat
The model uses a **60/40 debt/equity comparison structure** across all three options for comparability. This is useful analytically, but it should not be read as the likely financing outcome for a speculative Ioannina project. In practice:
- speculative Powered Shell may require **50/50 or lower leverage**, and
- Build-to-exit may require even more conservative financing until leasing becomes visible.

### 4.2 Meaning of the results

#### Turnkey
- Highest capex and highest income potential.
- Strong upside if leasing and pricing work.
- Worst downside because a rent miss or delay hurts a much larger capital base.
- Base case is attractive at the asset level, but downside becomes clearly weak.

#### Powered Shell
- Lowest capex and lowest break-even rent.
- Base case returns are strong enough.
- Downside case remains far more survivable than Turnkey.
- Best fit for optionality and for a thinner market like Ioannina.

#### Build-to-exit
- Strongest deterministic IRR because value is crystallized earlier.
- But the result depends heavily on being able to lease up and sell on time.
- It is more fragile than the headline base-case IRR suggests.

### 4.3 Important caveat on DSCR
All three strategies show weak minimum DSCR values under the flat 60/40 leverage assumption, especially in downside cases. This means:
- the **strategy comparison is still useful**,
- but the **financing structure is not yet lender-grade**.

This is especially important for Powered Shell and Build-to-exit. If the sponsor wants a financing-ready model, it should test:
- lower leverage,
- interest-only grace periods,
- sculpted debt service,
- or anchor-led pre-let cases.

## 5. SWOT analysis

### 5.1 Turnkey

**Strengths**
- Highest absolute NOI once stabilized.
- Stronger institutional sale story if leased.
- Best fit for anchor-led, compliance-heavy, sovereign or regulated users.
- More bankable if a strong tenant is signed.

**Weaknesses**
- Highest capex at risk.
- Most vulnerable to design mismatch.
- Hardest strategy to reverse after committing tenant-ready fit-out.

**Opportunities**
- Anchor-led BTS.
- Sovereign, public-sector, regulated workloads.
- Premium pricing if speed and customization matter to the tenant.

**Threats**
- Over-fit-out before demand is real.
- Lease-up delays.
- Yield widening.
- Tenant-specific redesign risk.

### 5.2 Powered Shell

**Strengths**
- Lowest capital at risk.
- Best optionality.
- Easiest to adapt as market feedback evolves.
- Best fit with a credible but thinner secondary-market location.

**Weaknesses**
- Lower immediate NOI.
- Some occupiers may prefer more delivered scope.
- Pure speculative shell is not as lender-friendly as leased turnkey.

**Opportunities**
- Regional operator, MSP, cloud, and carrier-neutral demand.
- Conversion to turnkey later if a strong anchor emerges.
- Better fit with phased commercialization.

**Threats**
- Under-monetization if a premium tenant appears and the sponsor reacts too slowly.
- Tenant fit-out coordination risk.

### 5.3 Build-to-exit

**Strengths**
- Highest headline base-case IRR.
- Shorter sponsor capital duration.
- Attractive if management's real goal is capital recycling.

**Weaknesses**
- Highly sensitive to timing.
- More dependent on exit yield than hold strategies.
- Less forgiving if lease-up slips into the sale window.

**Opportunities**
- Could work well if a credible operator or tenant is signed and the project is visibly de-risked before sale.

**Threats**
- Ioannina is not a proven prime exit market.
- Buyer pool may be thinner than in established cloud regions.
- Sale value can fall sharply if yields widen.

## 6. Risk analysis

### 6.1 Core risk register

| Risk | Most exposed option | Why it matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lease-up delay | Turnkey / Build-to-exit | Ioannina has thinner public evidence of deep multi-MW absorption | Anchor-first marketing, staged commercialization |
| Pricing miss | Turnkey | High capex requires stronger rent to clear hurdle | Avoid speculative fit-out without tenant visibility |
| Exit-yield widening | Build-to-exit | Sale thesis is highly value-sensitive | Keep exit optional, not mandatory |
| Bankability / leverage | Powered Shell / Build-to-exit | Speculative structures may not support 60/40 financing | Lower leverage until pre-let |
| Scope mismatch | Turnkey | Wrong design specification can trap capex | Keep base design modular |
| Counterparty risk | Build-to-exit / Powered Shell | Secondary-market tenants may be weaker than hyperscalers | Parent guarantees, deposits, step-in rights |
| Power / schedule slip | All | Even secured projects still carry execution risk | Preserve float and stage commitments |
| Small buyer pool | Build-to-exit | Ioannina is not yet a prime exit market | Underwrite hold case as fallback |
| Under-monetization | Powered Shell | Too much conservatism can leave value on the table | Pre-engineer turnkey conversion path |

### 6.2 Risk ranking
- **Lowest downside risk:** Powered Shell
- **Highest execution risk:** Turnkey
- **Highest market / exit risk:** Build-to-exit

## 7. Monte Carlo output and interpretation

### 7.1 Monte Carlo setup
The Monte Carlo uses triangular distributions around low / base / high assumptions for:
- rent,
- capex,
- opex,
- debt cost,
- exit yield,
- occupancy.

This is appropriate because the Ioannina case does not yet have a deep public transaction history that would justify more complex probability distributions.

### 7.2 Output

| Option | NPV P10 | NPV P50 | NPV P90 | Equity IRR P10 | Equity IRR P50 | Equity IRR P90 | Probability NPV < 0 | Probability Equity IRR < 15% |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Turnkey | €7.0m | €16.6m | €26.2m | 14.1% | 16.2% | 18.2% | 0.8% | 23.6% |
| Powered Shell | €10.2m | €15.4m | €21.0m | 16.6% | 18.6% | 20.6% | 0.0% | 0.9% |
| Build-to-exit | -€3.8m | €4.8m | €14.5m | 9.4% | 16.6% | 24.0% | 25.5% | 38.8% |

### 7.3 Interpretation

#### Turnkey
Turnkey has respectable upside, but a materially higher probability of missing the 15% equity hurdle. This reflects the larger capital-at-risk and the dependence on stronger pricing and lease-up.

#### Powered Shell
Powered Shell has the strongest risk-adjusted profile. It gives up some upside, but the downside is much shallower and the hurdle-miss probability is minimal.

#### Build-to-exit
Build-to-exit wins the deterministic base-case IRR contest, but loses the probabilistic contest. It is hit twice when things go wrong:
1. lease-up delay hurts NOI,
2. wider exit yields reduce the sale value.

This is why Build-to-exit should be treated as an **optional ownership strategy**, not the core recommendation.

## 9. Board-level conclusion

### Recommendation
For the **single 12 MW Ioannina project**, the recommended strategy is:

## **Powered Shell with pre-engineered Turnkey convertibility**

### Why this is the best option now
- The project benefits from land ownership, permits, secured grid scope/cost, and a defined energization target.
- The broader European market is supportive, but Ioannina is still a thinner and less proven leasing and exit market than established cloud regions.
- Powered Shell minimizes speculative capex while preserving strategic flexibility.
- It performs best on a risk-adjusted basis in the deterministic and Monte Carlo analysis.

### When the recommendation changes
- **Turnkey becomes best** if a strong anchor pre-let is secured on acceptable commercial terms.
- **Build-to-exit becomes best** only if management deliberately targets capital recycling within a short time horizon and accepts higher timing and sale-risk exposure.

### Immediate actions for management
1. Position the scheme as a **Powered Shell product with Turnkey conversion capability**.
2. Start anchor-led tenant outreach immediately.
3. Re-run the model with:
   - lower leverage,
   - interest-only grace period,
   - and anchor-prelet cases.
4. Avoid committing full speculative Turnkey fit-out before tenant visibility is real.
5. Keep Build-to-exit as an option, not as the core business plan.

---

## 10. Final one-paragraph board answer

The Ioannina project should be advanced as a **Powered Shell development with pre-engineered Turnkey optionality**. This is the best match for a project that has real infrastructure readiness but sits in a market that is strategically credible rather than fully proven at 12 MW wholesale scale. Turnkey has the highest absolute upside once leased, but it is too exposed to pricing and lease-up risk to justify speculative execution today. Build-to-exit has attractive deterministic IRR, but it is materially more fragile in probabilistic terms because it depends on both lease-up and exit pricing working on time. The most disciplined path is therefore to preserve optionality, protect capital, and convert to Turnkey only if a credible anchor tenant makes that decision economically and strategically justified.
